**Capture fast, play slow.** That's the whole idea.

## The principle

Leave the **playback fps** unchanged on the timeline; **raise the capture fps**. The same event gets recorded across more frames, so on a 24/30fps timeline it plays back proportionally slower.

> **Slow-motion ratio = capture fps ÷ playback fps**

| Capture fps | 24fps timeline | 30fps timeline |
| ----------- | -------------- | -------------- |
| 60          | 2.5× slow      | 2× slow        |
| 120         | **5× slow**    | 4× slow        |
| 240         | 10× slow       | 8× slow        |
| 1000        | ~42× slow      | ~33× slow      |

## Lowering fps for slow-mo? — No

Easy to confuse.

- **Capture at lower fps** → time gaps between frames widen → fast motion turns into a **stuttering time-lapse**.
- **Capture at higher fps** → finer time slices → playback unrolls slowly.

Slow motion is **about slicing time finer**.

## The shutter rule applies to "capture fps"

The [[shutter-angle|180° rule]] still holds for slow-mo, but **recompute it against the capture fps**.

| Capture fps | 180° shutter speed |
| ----------- | ------------------ |
| 60          | 1/120              |
| 120         | 1/240              |
| 240         | 1/480              |

→ **Higher fps capture = faster shutter = more light required.** That's why slow-mo usually needs to happen in **bright environments**. It's also why indoor slow-mo is hard to expose properly.

## Common mistakes

1. **Leaving shutter alone while raising fps** — Shooting 60fps with 1/50 shutter is **effectively impossible** (shutter angle would exceed 360°), and the camera will silently shorten it. The look ends up off.
2. **Wrong timeline fps** — Putting a 120fps clip on a 120fps timeline gives **no slow motion**. The timeline should be 24/30, only the clip 120.
3. **Not enough motion blur** — Slow-mo with very fast shutter looks **unnaturally jittery** because each frame is too crisp. Unless that's the point, keep the angle near 180°.

## Practical examples

- **120fps + 24fps timeline** — the most common 5× slow-mo. Good for human movement, water droplets, sports.
- **240fps** — claps, fast hand gestures, splashes.
- **1000fps+** (specialized gear) — bullets, balloon pops, advertising.
