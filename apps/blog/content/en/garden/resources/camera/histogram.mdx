A **graph of brightness distribution** in a photo. The little chart in Lightroom's top-right. On the camera LCD too, it's the most reliable indicator for RAW exposure.

## Reading the graph

```
pixel count
↑
│       ╭──╮
│     ╭─╯  ╰─╮
│   ╭─╯       ╰╮
│ ╭─╯           ╰─╮
└─┴───────────────┴───→ brightness
  0                255
  black            white
```

- **X-axis**: dark (0) ↔ bright (255)
- **Y-axis**: number of pixels at that brightness
- **Where peaks sit**: where the image's tones cluster

## Four regions ↔ four sliders

The four sliders in [[dynamic-range]] map one-to-one onto histogram regions.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Blacks │ Shadows │ ★ Mid ★ │ Highlights │ Whites │
│  0–10  │  10–30  │  30–70  │   70–90    │ 90–100 │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   left edge ───────────────────────────── right edge
```

Hovering the cursor over Lightroom's histogram **highlights each region and shows you which slider corresponds to it**. Learning this one feature alone tightens up editing.

## Clipping warnings

The **little triangles** at the upper corners of the histogram.

- **Left triangle lit (blue)**: clipping in blacks. Pixels lost into pure black.
- **Right triangle lit (red)**: clipping in whites. Pixels blown to pure white.

**Click a triangle** and the clipped pixels get **highlighted as a colored overlay** on the image, so you can see exactly where it died.

> **Shortcut J**: toggle the clipping overlay. You'll press it constantly while editing.

## Is there a "good" histogram shape?

**No fixed answer.** A dark scene rightly has its peaks toward the left; a snowy landscape rightly has them on the right.

But as guidelines:

- **Reach the edges without clipping** — if the histogram is collapsed in the middle and never touches the edges, the photo feels flat. Letting both ends just barely reach the edges adds richness.
- **Watch for peaks pasted against the wall** — if a peak is jammed against the right edge (highlight clipping), there's no data left to recover.

## Expose to the Right — RAW capture strategy

> **When shooting RAW, push exposure to just shy of clipping.**

Why: digital sensors **allocate more steps to the bright zones**. Lifting from shadows brings noise with it, but pulling down from highlights is nearly lossless.

```
Tonal steps a sensor records (14-bit basis)
Brightest stop:  8192 steps  ←─── information-dense
Next stop:       4096 steps
Next:            2048 steps
Next:            1024 steps
Darkest stop:      64 steps  ←─── information-poor (noise dominates)
```

→ **Capture slightly bright (just before clipping)** → **bring it down in post**.
You end up with cleaner shadows.

This only matters in RAW + post. JPEG output gives you no such benefit.

## RGB-separated histogram

Lightroom's histogram by default overlays the red/green/blue channels. Click to view them separately.

- **Only red clipping** → a too-intense sunset, or skin pushed too red
- **Blue piled at the dark end** → low blue in shadows → image leans warm
- **All three peak at the same spot** → image leans toward neutral gray

## Why the camera's LCD histogram isn't fully accurate

The histogram on your camera at capture is based on the **JPEG preview**, not the RAW.

- Things that look clipped in JPEG can still hold detail in RAW (about a 1-stop margin).
- A mild clipping warning on the LCD is often recoverable in RAW.
- Trust the **RAW histogram in Lightroom** for the final word.

## A practical edit order with the histogram

1. **Open the histogram**, set Exposure so peaks land roughly where you want them
2. **Check the clipping triangles** — if red/blue are lit, hit J to see where
3. **Highlights / Shadows** to recover both ends
4. **Whites / Blacks** to define the end-points (touch the walls just barely)
5. With tone in place, move on to color: [[white-balance|WB]], [[hsl-and-color-mixer|HSL]], [[tone-curve|curve]]
