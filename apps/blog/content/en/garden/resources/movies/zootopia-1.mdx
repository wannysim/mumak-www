A Disney animated film. The animal-city setting pulls you in fast, and the story about prejudice turns out to be far more direct than the premise suggests.

## Key Themes

- **Nick Wilde**: The standout character. The cable car scene — where he opens up about the memory that shaped him — is where the film earns its emotional weight. And the second half, after the rift between him and Judy, when they choose to trust each other again, is the moment the partnership means something.
- **Prejudice without softening**: The film doesn't dress it up. Nick's childhood flashback shows exactly how prejudice gets planted and calcifies. The civil unrest after Judy's press conference shows how quickly it spreads again. It felt like a frank portrait of how people actually behave.
- **Judy seeing past the fox**: The early scene where Judy watches Nick with his child — and what she notices is not the species but the warmth of a father — is small, but it sets everything else in motion.
- **A world built for more**: Even in the first film, a train passing through the city briefly shows how many districts exist. It read at the time as deliberate: a world designed with room to expand. The sequel confirmed it, using the districts only glimpsed here as its actual setting.

## Summary

The worldbuilding works because it serves the argument, not just the spectacle. And Nick's cable car scene is one of the more honest pieces of emotional writing in a Disney film in a long time.
