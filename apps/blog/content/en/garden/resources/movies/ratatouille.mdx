A Pixar animated film. The first time I watched it, I mostly came away with a vague longing for Paris and the warmth of Remy and Linguini's friendship. It took time to see what the film was really doing.

## Key Themes

- **Remy as the true protagonist**: His story is the one that carries the film's central theme most completely. The passion of a creature willing to risk his life for the chance to cook — that kind of uncompromising commitment to a dream was a genuine inspiration growing up.
- **The synesthetic food scene**: Remy describing strawberries and cheese to Emile using bursts of color and sound. It is the film's clearest statement about what it means to truly taste something — and it still comes to mind whenever I eat something unexpected.
- **Anton Ego's final monologue**: What takes time to appreciate. A critic who spent his career dismissing things publicly acknowledges his own narrowness, and he does it in prose that is both precise and warm — like the writing of someone who has just been reminded of his mother's kitchen. The register alone makes the scene work.
- **"Anyone can cook" completed**: Gusteau's phrase is idealistic. Ego's closing words are the harder version: not that anyone will cook well, but that the next great chef could come from anywhere. The two lines together are what the film is actually arguing.

## Summary

What I missed the first time was the film's argument. What stays now is the image of Ego at his desk, writing in a different voice than he ever had before.
